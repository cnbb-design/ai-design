# Quick Definition

A typographic symbol (typo-symbol) is a logo or identifying mark designed from typographic elements — rules, geometric shapes, and letterforms from the compositor's case — rather than drawn by hand, gaining its strength from "the technical manufacturing process" and offering advantages of easy reproduction, scaling, and no block costs.

# Core Definition

Tschichold defines the requirements: "A good symbol is... the expression of a unified design for advertising and manufacture. It must be original and simple in form, have a very high degree of memorability, and be easily recognized and noticeable."

He distinguishes the typographic approach: "It is also possible to design symbols, sometimes even more effectively, by using typography. The advantage of such 'typo-symbols' are: no block costs, the facility for reduction and enlargement, and the strength inherent in all things whose appearance comes from a technical manufacturing process."

The raw materials: "The means available to the printer for designing symbols are the typographic contents of the composing-room: rules, straight and curved, geometrical shapes, letters and — not least — imagination."

He cautions sharply against substituting monograms for true symbols: "A symbol is something completely different and of higher importance than a monogram, and a faulty symbol is far worse than none at all."

# Prerequisites

- **Functional Typography** — Symbol design applies functional principles: the mark must communicate, not decorate.

# Key Properties

1. **Originality and simplicity**: Must be novel in form yet reducible to essentials.
2. **High memorability**: The test of a symbol is instant recognition.
3. **Technical strength**: Typographic construction gives a machine-made precision that hand-drawing cannot.
4. **Scalability**: Type-symbols can be reduced or enlarged without loss.
5. **No block costs**: Composed from existing typographic material, not requiring custom blocks.
6. **Symbol vs. monogram**: A monogram is not a symbol — "a monogram as the symbol of a product is always worse than a proper symbol."

# Construction / Recognition

## To Construct/Create:
1. Work from typographic material: rules (straight and curved), geometric shapes, letterforms.
2. Aim for maximum simplicity and originality — "the difficulties of making a good symbol should only be a stimulus to their overcoming."
3. The symbol should either combine letters into a new form, illustrate the product, or do both.
4. Test for memorability and recognisability at multiple scales.

## To Identify/Recognise:
1. Typo-symbol: constructed from typographic elements, with machine-precision quality.
2. Drawn symbol: relies on the artist's hand; effective but technically constrained.
3. Monogram: merely combines initials — insufficient as a true symbol.

# Context & Application

- **Typical contexts**: Corporate identity, product branding, trade marks, printer's devices.
- **Common applications**: Logo design, brand marks, favicon design, app icons.
- **Historical note**: The typo-symbol tradition developed alongside modernist graphic design in the 1920s. The principle of constructing marks from geometric and typographic elements influenced the systematic corporate identity programmes of the mid-20th century (e.g., Paul Rand, Josef Müller-Brockmann).

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 147): Kurt Schwitters's Pelikan symbol — "the effect of the Pelikan symbol depends on the use of the simplest type-forms in strong contrast and movement."

**Example 2** (p. 148): Piet Zwart's personal mark — constructed from the initial P and a black square ("Zwart" = Dutch for black), creating "an unforgettable device."

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Substituting a type monogram for a true symbol.
  **Correction**: "A symbol is something completely different and of higher importance than a monogram, and a faulty symbol is far worse than none at all."

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Any combination of letters makes a symbol.
  **Clarification**: A symbol requires originality, simplicity, and memorability. "So truly effective symbols are now doubly rare" — most attempts fail these criteria.
