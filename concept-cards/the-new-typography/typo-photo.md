# Quick Definition

Typo-photo is the synthesis of typography and photography into a unified graphic form — "the collective form of graphic art" that replaces the older "handwriting-drawing" paradigm, achieving its effect through the contrast between flat type and three-dimensional photographic imagery.

# Core Definition

Tschichold coins the term: "By typo-photo we mean any synthesis between typography and photography. Today we can express ourselves better and more quickly with the help of photography than by the laborious means of speech or writing."

The synthesis has a formal basis: "there is only one objective type form — sanserif — and only one objective representation of our times: photography. Hence typo-photo, as the collective form of graphic art, has today taken over from the individualistic form handwriting-drawing."

The key aesthetic principle is contrast: "At first sight it seems as if the hard black forms of this typeface could not harmonize with the often soft greys of photos. The two together do not have the same weight of colour: their harmony lies in the contrast of form and colour. But both have two things in common: their objectivity and their impersonal form."

He further argues: "Purely flat typography belongs to the past. The introduction of the photographic block has enabled us to use the dynamics of three dimensions."

# Key Properties

1. **Synthesis, not juxtaposition**: Photography and type form a unified composition.
2. **Contrast-based harmony**: Flat type vs. three-dimensional photography; hard black vs. soft grey.
3. **Objectivity**: Both sans-serif and photography are "objective" and impersonal.
4. **Photography as type element**: The photographic block "joins letters and rules in the compositor's case as a contemporary but differentiated typographic element."
5. **Three dimensions**: Photography introduces spatial depth into the flat typographic plane.
6. **Sans-serif + photography**: The natural pairing — "the problem of type never actually existed: it had to be sanserif."

# Construction / Recognition

## To Construct/Create:
1. Integrate photographic images as compositional elements equal to type, not as illustrations subordinate to text.
2. Use the formal contrast between flat type and photographic depth as the compositional driver.
3. Pair sans-serif type with photography for maximum objectivity.
4. Use colour (especially red) to enhance the relationship between type and photo.

## To Identify/Recognise:
1. Typo-photo: photography and type composed as a unified visual whole.
2. Old approach: photos treated as insertions into a text layout, separate from the typographic composition.

# Context & Application

- **Typical contexts**: Advertising, editorial design, posters, book covers.
- **Common applications**: Modern editorial design, web hero sections combining photography and typography, social media graphics.
- **Historical note**: The concept was developed simultaneously by Moholy-Nagy (who coined "typophoto" in 1925) and Tschichold. It became the dominant paradigm for advertising and editorial design throughout the 20th century.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 90): Piet Zwart's NKF advertisement — "The typography and the photo echo each other: the centre of NKF with the centre of the cable cross-section, the red lines of type with the cable circumference."

**Example 2** (p. 84): El Lissitzky's photogram advertisement for Pelikan ink — "Even the lettering is photo-mechanical."

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Treating photographs as decorative additions to an otherwise complete typographic layout.
  **Correction**: The photograph must be an integrated compositional element, not a tacked-on illustration.

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Sans-serif type and photographs cannot harmonise because of their different visual weights.
  **Clarification**: "Their harmony lies in the contrast of form and colour. But both have two things in common: their objectivity and their impersonal form."
