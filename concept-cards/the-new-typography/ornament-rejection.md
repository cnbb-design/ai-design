# Quick Definition

All typographic ornament — decorative rules, printers' flowers, "abstract decorations," and even the thick/thin rule — must be eliminated because decoration reveals "a fear of pure appearance" and obscures the typographic function of communication.

# Core Definition

Tschichold, citing architect Adolf Loos (1898): "The more primitive a people, the more extravagantly they use ornament and decoration... To seek beauty in form itself rather than make it dependent on ornament should be the aim of all mankind."

Applied to typography: "The use of ornament, in whatever style or quality, comes from an attitude of childish naivety. It shows a reluctance to use 'pure design,' a giving-in to a primitive instinct to decorate — which reveals, in the last resort, a fear of pure appearance. It is so easy to employ ornament to cover up bad design!"

The scope of "ornament" is broader than expected: "'Ornament' is not only decorated rules and printers' flowers but also includes all combinations of rules. Even the thick/thin rule is an ornament, and must be avoided." Also: "'Abstract decorations' which some foundries have produced under different names are also ornaments in this sense."

# Prerequisites

- **Functional Typography** — Ornament rejection follows from the principle that form must serve function; decoration serves only appearance.

# Key Properties

1. **Broad definition**: Ornament includes decorated rules, flowers, thick/thin rules, "abstract decorations," and geometric shapes used without function.
2. **Covers old and new**: Both traditional floral ornaments and "elementary ornaments" (circles, triangles used decoratively) are rejected.
3. **Childish naivety**: Decoration reveals fear of pure form and reluctance to let design stand on its own.
4. **Covers up bad design**: Ornament is used to disguise poor compositional decisions.
5. **Not mere omission**: Simply removing ornament (as in the "Das politische Buch" letterhead) is not enough — functional form must replace it.

# Construction / Recognition

## To Construct/Create:
1. Remove all decorative elements — rules, borders, flourishes, abstract shapes without function.
2. Replace decorative emphasis with functional emphasis (contrast, spacing, weight).
3. If a geometric shape (circle, triangle, rule) is used, it must serve a function — emphasis, grouping, or directional guidance.
4. Test: remove the element. Does the communication suffer? If not, the element was decorative.

## To Identify/Recognise:
1. Ornamental design: visual elements that serve appearance but not communication.
2. Functional design: every visual element serves the reading experience.

# Context & Application

- **Typical contexts**: Evaluating any design for decorative excess.
- **Common applications**: Modern flat design, minimal UI, content-first web design all inherit this principle.
- **Historical note**: Loos published "Ornament und Verbrechen" (Ornament and Crime) in 1908. Tschichold applies the argument specifically to typography in 1928. The principle was moderated over the century — postmodernism reintroduced ornament, and current practice acknowledges decorative elements can serve emotional and cultural functions.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 75): "The thick/thin rule... was used to disguise contrasts, to reduce them to one level. The New Typography, on the other hand, emphasizes contrasts and uses them to create a new unity."

**Example 2** (p. 76): "Unfortunately many people have thought the essence of the New Typography consists merely in the use of bold rules, circles, and triangles" — substituting geometric ornament for traditional ornament is no improvement.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Replacing traditional ornaments (flowers, borders) with "modern" ornaments (circles, bold rules, triangles).
  **Correction**: "If these are merely substituted for the old ornaments, nothing is improved." Geometric shapes must be functional.

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Ornament rejection means all visual elements besides type must go.
  **Clarification**: Rules, shapes, and colour are legitimate when they serve a communicative function — emphasis, contrast, grouping, direction. Only purposeless decoration is rejected.
