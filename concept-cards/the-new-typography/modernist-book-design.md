# Quick Definition

The modernist book replaces the Renaissance ideal of "a book page of even grey" with a new harmony built from "contrasting typographic and photographic elements using brightness, space, and direction" — integrating photography, asymmetric typography, and contemporary typefaces into a book form suited to the speed and visual orientation of modern reading.

# Core Definition

Tschichold describes a revolution: "In the area of book design, in the last few years a revolution has taken place... It means placing much greater emphasis on the appearance of the book and a wholly contemporary use of typographic and photographic means."

The formal principle: "The Renaissance ideal — a book page of even grey — is finished; instead we have harmony from contrasting typographic and photographic elements using brightness, space, and direction. Contemporary bindings and book-jackets similarly show an increasing use of photography."

He traces the development from Marinetti's typographic experiments through Lissitzky's poetry books to Heartfield's photomontage covers, establishing that "blocks, whether from photographs or drawings, have become an integrated part of the book" — where previously "the artists of the pre-war period denied the possibility of artistic unity in books using halftone blocks."

The practical demands: "(1) a contemporary (therefore not historical) typographic form; (2) inexpensive books for people, not luxury books for snobs."

# Key Properties

1. **Contrast replaces uniformity**: Harmony from contrasting elements (type sizes, photography, white space) rather than even grey pages.
2. **Photography as book element**: "The photographic block is being introduced into the book as a new element."
3. **Contemporary typefaces**: Sans-serif for display; functional roman for text when sans-serif is unavailable in sufficient quantity.
4. **DIN standard formats**: Book sizes follow DIN 476 — A5 for handbooks, A4 for art books, A6 for smaller books.
5. **Economy over luxury**: Affordable books for broad readership, not precious objects for collectors.
6. **Unprinted space as element**: "Unprinted space, which formerly meant nothing... is now as much a proper element as printed matter."

# Construction / Recognition

## To Construct/Create:
1. Choose DIN-standard book format appropriate to the content type.
2. Use contemporary (non-historical) typefaces — sans-serif for display, functional roman for text.
3. Integrate photographs and typographic elements as compositional equals.
4. Design pages through contrast of brightness, space, and direction rather than uniform grey.
5. Design covers using photography and typography, not ornamental illustration.

## To Identify/Recognise:
1. Modernist book: asymmetric layout, photographic elements integrated, contemporary type, DIN-format.
2. Traditional book: centred layout, even grey pages, historical typefaces, ornamental title pages.

# Context & Application

- **Typical contexts**: Book design, publication layout, editorial design, cover design.
- **Common applications**: Contemporary book and magazine layout, digital publication design, e-book design, web-based long-form content.
- **Historical note**: Key practitioners cited: Marinetti (typographic experimentation, 1919), Lissitzky (Mayakovsky poetry book, 1922-23), Heartfield (photomontage covers for Malik), Teige (Czech book design), Moholy-Nagy (Bauhaus books). Tschichold later moderated his views in *Typographische Gestaltung* (1935), moving toward a more classical approach while retaining the principle of functional design.

# Examples

**Example 1** (pp. 219-220): Lissitzky's Mayakovsky book — "The first pages of the poems are designed as pictorial paraphrases of their contents, as an optical expression of the spoken word. By means of a thumb index, as in a letter-file, a particular poem can be quickly found."

**Example 2** (p. 221): Heartfield's Malik covers — "He is the inventor of photomontage bindings. His Malik-bindings are the first of their kind."

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Applying modernist surface features to fundamentally traditional book structures.
  **Correction**: Tschichold insists the traditional book form "is perfectly suitable for this kind of book" (novels, scientific literature) — only modify where genuine improvement is possible, not for novelty's sake.

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: The "new book" means every book must be radically redesigned.
  **Clarification**: "It is not a question of making a really new kind of book (in a technical sense), because the old book-form is perfectly suitable for this kind of book" — modification is appropriate; revolution is for books whose content demands it (artist's books, photography books).
