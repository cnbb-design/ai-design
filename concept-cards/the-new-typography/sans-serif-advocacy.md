# Quick Definition

Tschichold argues that sans-serif ("Grotesque" or "block letter") is "the only one in spiritual accordance with our time" — its plainness, clarity, and geometric construction express the same values as modern architecture, making it the mandatory typeface for contemporary typography.

# Core Definition

Tschichold's argument is absolute: "None of the typefaces to whose basic form some kind of ornament has been added (serifs in roman type, lozenge shapes and curlicues in fraktur) meet our requirements for clarity and purity. Among all the types that are available, the so-called 'Grotesque' (sanserif) or 'block letter' ('skeleton letters' would be a better name) is the only one in spiritual accordance with our time."

He links typeface to architecture: "To proclaim sanserif as the typeface of our time is not a question of being fashionable, it really does express the same tendencies to be seen in our architecture."

Yet he acknowledges imperfection: "There is no doubt that the sanserif types available today are not yet wholly satisfactory as all-purpose faces." He praises Paul Renner's Futura as "a significant step in the right direction" while noting that "all the attempts up to now... are merely 'improvements' on the previous sanserifs: they are all still too artistic, too artificial."

For body text: "The main reason why sanserif is so seldom used today for normal text setting is that in general there is not enough of it available." When sans-serif is unavailable, he recommends "a good roman, and sanserif will be reserved for emphasis."

# Key Properties

1. **Only typeface of our time**: Sans-serif alone meets requirements for clarity and purity.
2. **Serifs are ornament**: Serifs, like fraktur decorations, are ornamental additions to the basic letter form.
3. **Architecture parallel**: Sans-serif expresses the same spirit as modern architecture.
4. **Not yet perfected**: Available sans-serifs are "not yet wholly satisfactory" — the ideal typeface will come from a group, including an engineer.
5. **Ranking**: (1) Jobbing sans-serifs, (2) Venus and copies (worse), (3) "painterly" block letters (third choice).
6. **Roman as fallback**: When sans-serif is unavailable, use plain romans (Sorbonne, Nordische Antiqua) — never decorative or historical faces.
7. **Set this book in sans-serif**: Tschichold used sans-serif for the entire book to demonstrate its readability.

# Construction / Recognition

## To Construct/Create:
1. Use sans-serif for all contemporary typographic work.
2. When sans-serif body text is unavailable, use a plain, "uninteresting" roman.
3. Reserve bold and semi-bold sans-serif for emphasis and display.
4. Avoid "artists' typefaces," historical faces, script, and decorated types.

## To Identify/Recognise:
1. The New Typography in practice: sans-serif type, often in multiple weights for hierarchy.
2. Old typography: decorated types, fraktur, ornamental romans.

# Context & Application

- **Typical contexts**: Any typographic design decision.
- **Common applications**: Sans-serif dominance in digital interfaces, branding, and signage.
- **Historical note**: Tschichold's advocacy was enormously influential. Sans-serif became the default for modernist design and later for digital interfaces. He specifically praised Futura (1927), which remains in wide use. His later career saw him designing in traditional roman for book typography (Sabon, 1967), effectively contradicting his 1928 absolutism.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Using historical typefaces (fraktur, gothic, ornamental romans) in contemporary design.
  **Correction**: "It is absurd today to build villas like Rococo palaces or Gothic castles, so people tomorrow will smile at those who continue to practise the old typography."

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Sans-serif is advocated because it is fashionable or novel.
  **Clarification**: "To proclaim sanserif as the typeface of our time is not a question of being fashionable, it really does express the same tendencies to be seen in our architecture."
