# Quick Definition

Typography must be designed around how text is actually read — in successive steps from the most prominent element to less important ones — not according to a fixed left-to-right, top-to-bottom rule, because "it is shown at its clearest that we read most printed matter in successive steps."

# Core Definition

Tschichold distinguishes between the conventional reading direction and actual reading behaviour: "The typographer must take the greatest care to study how his work is read and ought to be read. It is true that we usually read from top left to bottom right — but this is not a law."

He describes how readers actually encounter printed matter: "It is shown at its clearest in Willi Baumeister's invitation card. There is no doubt that we read most printed matter in successive steps: first the heading (which need not be the opening word) and then, if we continue to read the printed matter at all, we read the rest bit by bit according to its importance."

The practical implication: "It is therefore quite feasible to start reading a text at a different point from the top left. The exact place depends entirely on the kind of printed matter and the text itself." But with a caution: "one must therefore, in general, not set a following body of text higher than the preceding one."

# Key Properties

1. **Not a fixed rule**: Top-left to bottom-right is a convention, not a law.
2. **Successive steps**: Readers scan for the most prominent element first, then proceed by importance.
3. **Heading first**: The heading draws the eye first — and it "need not be the opening word."
4. **Content-dependent**: "The exact place depends entirely on the kind of printed matter and the text itself."
5. **General caution**: Following text should generally not be placed higher than preceding text.
6. **Speed of modern reading**: "As a rule we no longer read quietly line by line, but glance quickly over the whole."

# Construction / Recognition

## To Construct/Create:
1. Identify the entry point — what should the reader see first?
2. Design the visual hierarchy so the eye moves from most important to least important.
3. Use type size, weight, and position to guide the reading sequence.
4. Test by glancing at the piece — where does your eye go first? Does it follow the intended sequence?

## To Identify/Recognise:
1. Good reading order: the eye is guided naturally through the content hierarchy.
2. Poor reading order: confusion about where to start or how to continue (e.g., the poster with "ten sizes of type" where "it is very difficult to find where to go on reading").

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 70): Willi Baumeister's invitation card — "There is no doubt that we read most printed matter in successive steps."

**Example 2** (p. 143): The badly designed poster — "Where does one begin reading? or continue? Ten sizes of type are used when certainly six at most were needed."

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Forcing the reader to find their way through a layout that prioritises visual form over reading sequence.
  **Correction**: "Typographic form must be organic, it must evolve from the nature of the text" — let content hierarchy determine the visual sequence.

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: All text must start at the top left.
  **Clarification**: The entry point can be anywhere if the visual hierarchy makes the reading sequence clear. But departing from convention requires careful design.
