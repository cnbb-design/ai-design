# Quick Definition

The New Typography embraces standardization — especially DIN paper format standards — as an economic and aesthetic necessity, replacing the "chaos" of arbitrary sizes with rational systems that benefit users, printers, manufacturers, and society at large.

# Core Definition

Tschichold presents standardization as integral to modern typography: "The New Typography, in its concern to satisfy the needs of our own period and to make sure that every single piece of printing is in harmony with the present, has always taken the greatest interest in every move towards standardization."

The argument is both practical and philosophical: "The need for standardization, in whatever area, derives from the problems of today, which it aims to solve." The economic case: "standardization is a guarantee for the raising of quality and lowering of prices."

He traces the history from Lichtenberg (1796) through the French Revolution to Dr. Porstmann and the DIN system, establishing standardization as the rational culmination of centuries of format chaos.

# Prerequisites

- **Functional Typography** — Standardization is the application of functional principles to format, sizing, and production systems.

# Key Properties

1. **Economic necessity**: Standardization saves costs in production, storage, distribution, and filing.
2. **Aesthetic advantage**: Standard formats "give good page proportions" — their artistic possibilities "are unlimited."
3. **Comprehensive scope**: Covers paper sizes, letterhead layout, margins, column widths, type areas.
4. **Against arbitrary formats**: "It is usually a sign of incompetence when a non-standard format is insisted on 'for artistic reasons.'"
5. **Benefits all parties**: Users (simpler filing), printers (efficient production), manufacturers (fewer sizes), and tradesman (simplified stock).

# Construction / Recognition

## To Construct/Create:
1. Choose DIN/ISO standard formats as the starting point for any design project.
2. Build from standard sizes rather than inventing custom dimensions.
3. Consider the item's place in a system — how will it be filed, stored, mailed alongside other standard items?

## To Identify/Recognise:
1. Standardized design: items in uniform sizes that file and store together logically.
2. Non-standardized design: arbitrary sizes that create filing chaos and production waste.

# Context & Application

- **Typical contexts**: Paper sizes, document formats, business forms, publication layout.
- **Common applications**: ISO 216 paper sizes (A4, A5, etc.), design systems with standardized component sizes, responsive design breakpoints.
- **Historical note**: The DIN 476 paper standard was adopted in Germany in the 1920s and became the international ISO 216 standard, used worldwide except in North America. The principle of standardization extended from typography to become foundational in design systems thinking.

## Cross-Domain Connections

**Engineering → STRUCTURAL**: DIN paper standardization applies the same industrial standardization logic used in engineering — replacing arbitrary individual choices with rational systems. The parallel holds for the methodology (systematic, efficiency-driven) but the application domain (visual communication vs. manufacturing) differs.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Choosing custom formats for "artistic" reasons when standard formats exist.
  **Correction**: "It is usually a sign of incompetence when a non-standard format is insisted on 'for artistic reasons.'"

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Standardization constrains creativity.
  **Clarification**: "The artistic possibilities of standard formats... are unlimited." Standardization frees the designer from arbitrary format decisions to focus on content and composition.
