# Quick Definition

Typographic contrast — the systematic use of opposing visual properties (large/small, light/dark, horizontal/vertical, coloured/plain) — is the primary means of creating visual hierarchy and meaning in functional typography, where "large differences in weight are better than small."

# Core Definition

Tschichold elevates contrast to a fundamental design principle: "The real meaning of form is made clearer by its opposite. We would not recognize day as day if night did not exist. The ways to achieve contrast are endless; the simplest are large/small, light/dark, horizontal/vertical, square/round, smooth/rough, closed/open, coloured/plain; all offer many possibilities of effective design."

His practical guidance is emphatic: "Large differences in weight are better than small. The closer in size different types are to each other, the weaker will be the result." And: "A limit to the number of type sizes used — normally three to not more than five — is always to be recommended."

The principle serves function: "Every part of a text relates to every other part by a definite, logical relationship of emphasis and value, predetermined by content. It is up to the typographer to express this relationship clearly and visibly, through type sizes and weight, arrangement of lines, use of colour, photography, etc."

# Key Properties

1. **Contrast creates meaning**: Form is made clear by its opposite.
2. **Seven contrast pairs**: large/small, light/dark, horizontal/vertical, square/round, smooth/rough, closed/open, coloured/plain.
3. **Large differences preferred**: "The closer in size different types are to each other, the weaker will be the result."
4. **Limit type sizes**: Three to five sizes maximum.
5. **Logical, not arbitrary**: "All form must correspond with meaning and not contradict it."
6. **Black-white-red**: "The already strong contrast between black and white can be greatly enhanced by the addition of red."

# Construction / Recognition

## To Construct/Create:
1. Identify the logical hierarchy of the content (what is most important, what is secondary).
2. Assign emphatic size differences to express that hierarchy — headlines very large, body noticeably smaller.
3. Limit to 3-5 type sizes.
4. Reinforce hierarchy with weight, colour, and spatial positioning.
5. Verify: does every contrast correspond to a meaningful distinction in the content?

## To Identify/Recognise:
1. Strong contrast: clear visual hierarchy, immediate identification of importance levels.
2. Weak contrast: "everything looks the same" — insufficient differentiation between elements.

# Context & Application

- **Typical contexts**: Any typographic design — posters, web pages, documents, advertisements.
- **Common applications**: Heading hierarchies, emphasis systems, call-to-action prominence.
- **Historical note**: Tschichold's contrast principles anticipate modern design systems that define explicit type scales with large ratio steps.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 72): "It is vital that all contrasts, for example in type sizes, should be logical. For example, a forename should not have a much larger initial letter if the beginning of the principal name is not specially indicated."

**Example 2** (p. 76): The poster redesign demonstration — "The effect is gained chiefly by contrasts in type sizes and the use of space. The large F used as eyecatcher. It can be seen how unimportant 'beautiful type' is."

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Using many type sizes with small differences between them.
  **Correction**: "A limit to the number of type sizes used — normally three to not more than five — is always to be recommended." Make differences emphatic.

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Strong contrast means visual chaos.
  **Clarification**: Contrast must be logical — every visual difference must correspond to a meaningful content distinction. Unlimited contrast without logic produces "pseudo-constructivism."
