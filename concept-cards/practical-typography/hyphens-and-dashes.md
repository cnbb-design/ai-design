# Quick Definition

Hyphens (-), en dashes (--), and em dashes (---) are three distinct marks with distinct functions: hyphens join compound words, en dashes indicate ranges and connections, and em dashes create parenthetical breaks in sentences.

# Core Definition

Butterick is direct: "Hyphens and dashes look similar, but they're not interchangeable." He identifies three marks with distinct purposes:

- **Hyphen (-)**: Line-break word splits, compound words (topsy-turvy), and phrasal adjectives (listener-supported radio).
- **En dash**: Ranges (1880--1912, pages 330--39) and connections/contrasts between word pairs (conservative--liberal split, Sarbanes--Oxley Act).
- **Em dash**: Breaks between parts of a sentence --- "Use it when a comma is too weak, but a colon, semicolon, or pair of parentheses is too strong."

Using multiple hyphens (-- or ---) to approximate dashes is "another typewriter habit. Use real dashes."

# Key Properties

1. **Three distinct marks**: Hyphen, en dash, em dash — each with specific uses.
2. **Not interchangeable**: Despite visual similarity.
3. **Em dash is underused**: "It is underused in professional writing."
4. **En dash for ranges**: 1880--1912. But: "If you open with *from*, pair it with *to*" (from 1880 to 1912, not from 1880--1912).
5. **Hyphen for phrasal adjectives**: five-dollar bills vs. five dollar bills — clarity depends on the hyphen.
6. **Named for em and en**: Typographic units, not the letters M and N.

# Construction / Recognition

## To Construct/Create:
1. **En dash**: Windows: alt+0150; Mac: option+hyphen; HTML: `&ndash;`
2. **Em dash**: Windows: alt+0151; Mac: option+shift+hyphen; HTML: `&mdash;`
3. Em and en dashes are typically set flush against surrounding text — add word spaces only if the font's built-in spacing feels cramped.
4. Never type -- or --- as a substitute.

## To Identify/Recognise:
1. **Hyphen**: shortest mark, used within or between words.
2. **En dash**: medium width (~half an em), used for ranges and word-pair connections.
3. **Em dash**: longest mark (~one em), used for sentence breaks.

# Context & Application

- **Typical contexts**: All typeset text — documents, web content, signage.
- **Common applications**: Compound words (hyphen); date and page ranges (en dash); parenthetical asides in prose (em dash).
- **Historical note**: "Em and en refer to units of typographic measurement, not to the letters M and N. (Yes, the homophony is confusing. To disambiguate, loud print shops referred to them as mutton and nut.)"

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Typing two hyphens (--) for an em dash or three (---) for emphasis.
  **Correction**: "Don't do that — it's another typewriter habit. Use real dashes." Insert proper en and em dash characters.

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: "Em" and "en" refer to the letters M and N.
  **Clarification**: They refer to units of typographic measurement. The em equals the point size of the font; the en is half that.
